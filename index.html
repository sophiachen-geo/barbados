Selling Paradise, Spending Health

On Barbados's Platinum Coast, real estate tourism is reshaping the land. Plantations and open ground are being turned into gated villas and golf greens. This is a story about how that transformation is connected to land use change, to the loss of public and green space, and to the island's rising burden of cardiovascular disease.

The question

Can the West Coast's real estate market coexist with sustainable, healthy communities?

Barbados is a Small Island Developing State whose sugar economy has, over fifty years, given way to resorts, villas and golf courses. This is the leisuring of rural landscapes, the conversion of land from productive to consumptive use. The project pairs a statistical analysis of luxury property listings and a week of field observation with open GIS layers, satellite imagery, and national health data to trace a single thread: how the supply side of real estate tourism reaches all the way into public space and public health.

01 · The island

A dense, fragile island economy

Barbados packs a dense population onto 11 parishes with limited resources and revenues that depend on imported goods and foreign visitors. Historic inequities left poor housing and an insecure land-tenure system, producing a highly uneven distribution of settlement that still shapes the island today.

The eleven parishes shown here are the spatial units that anchor every layer in this analysis.

02 · The Platinum Coast

Where Global Capital Meets the Shoreline

The West Coast, comprising Saint James and Saint Peter, is one of the most sought after real estate tourism locations in the Caribbean. Plantations have become resorts, villas and golf courses, all of them serving and exploiting a growing affluent class.

The 2020 Welcome Stamp lets non nationals earning over fifty thousand US dollars reside in Barbados tax free. It is the newest of many incentives drawing residential tourism to this coast.

03 · What the Listings Say

Pricing Privacy and Enclosure

A Python scraper pulled 135 listings from a local luxury portal. A multiple regression model then isolated what drives price. The strongest signals are not about the house at all. They are about enclosure and exclusivity.

Being inside a gated property adds 81 percent to the price. Each extra room adds another 25.5 percent. Penthouses and condominiums command large premiums of their own. The market literally pays for separation from the surrounding community.

04 · The Leisuring of the Land

From Productive Ground to Consumptive Amenity

Switch to satellite imagery and the West Coast corridor reads as near continuous built development hugging the shore. Agricultural land, in a country self sufficient only in chicken, has been converted to golf and polo. New projects climb higher and higher uphill on steep, erosion prone slopes.

The classified Landsat layers later in this story show exactly where that expansion concentrates. The highlighted corridor here is a hand drawn placeholder against the satellite basemap.

05 · The Loss of Public Space

Gates, Vacancy, and Unequal Water

Field visits mapped the texture of enclosure. Some hotels could be walked into freely. Others next door were camera guarded with manager only access. Foreign owned homes sit vacant for months or years even as locals live in overcrowded households.

Resource distribution tilts toward the coast. Hotels and tourist residences use three times more water than locals, forty percent of treated water never reaches customers, and raw sewage was observed on the East Coast but never on the West. Click the markers on the map to read each field encounter in full.

06 · The Obesogenic Environment

Built to Drive, Not to Move

The same land use pattern that produces sprawl, car dependence, and shrinking communal green space is what public health researchers call an obesogenic environment. The behavioural data are stark. Only 29 percent of thirteen to fifteen year olds are active for an hour on five or more days a week, while 65 percent are sedentary for three hours or more each day.

Navigating Barbados without a car is hard. Physical immobility becomes social immobility.

07 · The Health Signal

Heart Attacks Are Rising. Strokes Are Flat.

National registry data tell a sharp story. Between 2013 and 2020, acute heart attack incidence rose about 55 percent. The window coincides with the most recent wave of tourism driven development. Stroke incidence over the same period stayed essentially flat.

That divergence is the empirical hook tying the built environment to cardiovascular outcomes.

08 · The Mechanism

Hypertension, Diabetes, Obesity

The pathway runs through well documented risk factors. Among hospitalised heart attack patients, 86 percent had hypertension and 86 percent were obese. Eight in ten men and nine in ten women carry at least one chronic disease risk factor, and two of every three adults are overweight or obese.

Non communicable diseases now account for 83 percent of all deaths and consume 70 percent of the national hospital budget. That is the public cost of a privatised landscape.

09 · The Spatial Overlap

Bringing Disease Down to the Neighbourhood

The 2010 census records self reported heart disease, hypertension and diabetes for roughly six hundred enumeration districts, alongside dwelling vintage cohorts and unoccupied dwelling counts. The latter form a built in tourism pressure index.

Joined to the parish polygons shown here, and eventually to the finer enumeration district shapefile, the census lets us test whether the parishes losing the most open land also carry the heaviest chronic disease burden. The choropleth here is illustrative until that finer layer lands.

10 · The field record

One week, a dozen encounters

Every point on this map is a logged observation from March 1–7: a church that welcomes locals and tourists alike, a farmer selling eggs across from a five-star hotel, gated estates selling “the ideal Caribbean home experience,” a new hillside project branded “green” to lift its price.

Explore them — each marker carries the raw note and the issues it speaks to.

11 · The Baseline, 1984 to 1992

The Landscape Before the Boom

In the earliest Landsat record, sugarcane still covers the central plain. Natural vegetation holds the steep eastern slopes and the rolling north. Built area sits almost entirely within Bridgetown and a handful of small coastal villages. This is the pre leisuring landscape against which every later epoch is measured.

12 · The Mid Period, 2002 to 2010

The Tourism Real Estate Boom Registers in the Pixels

By the turn of the millennium, gated villa enclaves and resort fairways have begun to replace cropland along the western coast. Compare this layer to the baseline and the shift is unmistakable on the Platinum Coast strip running north from Holetown. The manicured vegetation signature also begins to spread inland.

13 · The Welcome Stamp Era, 2019 to 2025

The Current Map, and a Serious Caveat

This is the most recent classification in the Landsat record. Cross validation flagged that the thirty metre Landsat classifier achieves only 18.6% accuracy in Saint James, with a kappa of 0.03. That is the single most important parish for this project. The next step therefore offers a finer resolution sanity check built from Sentinel 2.

Read the parish cross validation table on the methods page.

14 · Cross Check at Ten Metres

A Finer Resolution Sanity Check

The same five class scheme, this time built from ten metre Sentinel 2 imagery. The increased resolution makes individual hotel footprints, road widths, and villa lot boundaries visible to the classifier. The two products should broadly agree where the Landsat classifier is reliable, and diverge informatively where it is not.

15 · What Became Urban

The Single Most Direct Conversion Map

Red pixels were anything other than built in the 1984 epoch and are built today. The pattern traces a coastal arc that is dense along the western shore, lighter along the south, and almost absent on the wilder east. Of all the layers in this stack, this is the one that speaks most directly to real estate driven urbanisation.

16 · Where the Sugar Went

The Geography of Plantation Decline

Barbados had over thirty thousand hectares under sugarcane at independence. Today the figure is under four thousand. This layer shows what each former sugarcane pixel became. The red Sugarcane to Built class concentrated along the western coast is the spatial signature of plantation land sold for tourism real estate.

17 · The Golf and Resort Fingerprint

The Manicured Vegetation Index

High values, shown in deep green, identify heavily managed turf. These are golf course fairways, resort lawns, and hotel grounds. The index exploits seasonal phenology: mowed grass has a flat seasonal signal that neither natural vegetation nor sugarcane cropping shows. Sandy Lane, Apes Hill, Royal Westmoreland, and the Sandals fairways all light up clearly.

18 · Greening or Browning

The NDVI Trend, 2017 to 2024

Red marks pixels where vegetation cover is decreasing year on year. Green marks the opposite. The western coast and the densifying suburban ring around Bridgetown show consistent browning over the most recent seven years of imagery. This is the most direct loss of green space layer in the entire stack.

19 · When the Change Happened

The Year of First Detected Change

Each pixel is coloured by the year a continuous change detection algorithm first registered a real disturbance. Purple indicates a change that happened decades ago, often the 1990s sugar decline. Yellow indicates a change in the past few years, the visual fingerprint of construction during the Welcome Stamp era. The temporal pattern here mirrors the rising incidence of heart attacks over the same window.

20 · Where the Classifiers Disagree

A Reliability Map

Five separate classifiers vote on each pixel. This layer shows the count of disagreements. High values mark pixels where the methods do not agree, and any per pixel statistic should be treated with care there. Read this alongside the parish cross validation table on the methods page.

The Finding This Project Is Built to Defend

As tourism driven development converted West Coast land from open and agricultural ground to built and enclosed estates, it hollowed out the public and active spaces that protect health. The parishes under the most pressure now sit alongside a steeply rising burden of cardiovascular disease.

Causal but not yet proven. The national cardiovascular trends are temporal, the census disease data are a 2010 cross section, and parish or enumeration district level incidence must still be requested from the Barbados National Registry. The spatial join described below is the test that turns this narrative into evidence.

On causation. This site presents an association and a mechanism, not a proven cause. The land use and health pathway it visualises is consistent with the WHO Global Syndemic framing, which names urban design and land use as drivers of obesity. Confirming the chain for Barbados specifically requires sub national cardiovascular incidence and controls for wealth, age, and healthcare access. The analysis is structured to accept exactly those data when they arrive.
