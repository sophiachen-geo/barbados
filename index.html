Selling Paradise, Spending Health

On Barbados's Platinum Coast, real estate tourism is reshaping the land — turning plantations and open ground into gated villas and golf greens. This is a story about how that transformation is connected to land-use change, the loss of public and green space, and the island's rising burden of cardiovascular disease.

The question

Can the West Coast's real estate market coexist with sustainable, healthy communities?

Barbados is a Small Island Developing State whose sugar economy has, over fifty years, given way to resorts, villas and golf courses — the “leisuring” of rural landscapes that converts land from productive to consumptive use. This project pairs a statistical analysis of luxury property listings and a week of field observation with open GIS layers, satellite imagery, and national health data to trace a single thread: how the supply side of real estate tourism reaches all the way into public space and public health.

01 · The island

A dense, fragile island economy

Barbados packs a dense population onto 11 parishes with limited resources and revenues that depend on imported goods and foreign visitors. Historic inequities left poor housing and an insecure land-tenure system, producing a highly uneven distribution of settlement that still shapes the island today.

The eleven parishes shown here are the spatial units that anchor every layer in this analysis.

02 · The Platinum Coast

Where global capital meets the shoreline

The West Coast — St James and St Peter — is one of the most sought-after real estate tourism locations in the Caribbean. Plantations have become resorts, villas and golf courses, “serving and exploiting” a growing affluent class.

The 2020 Welcome Stamp lets non-nationals earning over US$50,000 reside tax-free — the newest of many incentives drawing residential tourism to this coast.

03 · What the listings say

Pricing privacy and enclosure

A Python scraper pulled 135 listings from a local luxury portal; a multiple regression model isolated what drives price. The strongest signals are not about the house at all — they are about enclosure and exclusivity.

Being inside a gated property adds +81% to the price; each extra room adds +25.5%; penthouses and condos command large premiums. The market literally pays for separation from the surrounding community.

04 · Leisuring of the land

From productive ground to consumptive amenity

Switch to satellite imagery and the West Coast corridor reads as near-continuous built-up development hugging the shore. Agricultural land — Barbados is self-sufficient only in chicken — has been converted to golf and polo, while new projects climb higher and higher uphill on steep, erosion-prone slopes.

A satellite land-cover-change layer (1984→2020 Landsat / recent Sentinel-2) drops in here once classified; the highlighted corridor marks where that expansion concentrates.

05 · The loss of public space

Gates, vacancy, and unequal water

Field visits mapped the texture of enclosure. Some hotels could be walked into freely; others next door were camera-guarded with manager-only access. Foreign-owned homes sit vacant for months or years even as locals live in overcrowded households.

Resource distribution tilts to the coast: hotels and tourist residences use three times more water than locals, 40% of treated water never reaches customers, and raw sewage was seen on the East coast — never on the West. Click the markers to read each encounter.

06 · The obesogenic environment

Built to drive, not to move

The same land-use pattern — sprawl, car dependence, shrinking communal and green space — is what public-health researchers call an obesogenic environment. The behavioural data are stark: only 29% of 13–15-year-olds are active 60 minutes on 5+ days a week, while 65% are sedentary 3+ hours a day.

Navigating Barbados without a car is hard — physical immobility becomes social immobility.

07 · The health signal

Heart attacks are rising; strokes are flat

National registry data tell a sharp story. Between 2013 and 2020, acute heart-attack (AMI) incidence rose about 55% — the same window as the most recent wave of tourism-driven development. Stroke incidence, by contrast, stayed essentially flat.

That divergence is the empirical hook tying the built environment to cardiovascular outcomes.

08 · The mechanism

Hypertension, diabetes, obesity

The pathway runs through well-documented risk factors. Among hospitalised heart-attack patients, 86% had hypertension and 86% were obese; 8 in 10 men and 9 in 10 women carry at least one NCD risk factor; two of every three adults are overweight or obese.

Non-communicable diseases now account for 83% of all deaths and consume 70% of the national hospital budget — the public cost of a privatised landscape.

09 · The spatial overlap

Bringing disease down to the neighbourhood

The 2010 census records self-reported heart disease, hypertension and diabetes for roughly 600 enumeration districts, alongside dwelling-vintage cohorts and unoccupied-dwelling counts — a built-in tourism-pressure index.

Joined to the parish polygons shown here (and, once the ED shapefile is obtained, to the finer districts), this lets us test whether the parishes losing the most open land also carry the heaviest chronic-disease burden. The choropleth here is illustrative until your ED layer lands.

The finding this project is built to defend

As tourism-driven development converted West-Coast land from open and agricultural to built-up and enclosed, it hollowed out the public and active spaces that protect health — and the parishes under the most pressure now sit alongside a steeply rising burden of cardiovascular disease.

Causal, not yet proven: national CVD trends are temporal, the census disease data are a 2010 cross-section, and parish/ED-level incidence must still be requested from the Barbados National Registry. The spatial join below is the test that turns this narrative into evidence.

On causation. This site presents an association and a mechanism, not a proven cause. The land-use–health pathway it visualises is consistent with the WHO “Global Syndemic” framing — which names urban design and land use as drivers of obesity — but confirming it for Barbados requires sub-national CVD incidence and controls for wealth, age and healthcare access. The analysis is structured to accept exactly those data when they arrive.
